# Elinor Ostrom

Let me tell you about someone who changed what we know about how people govern themselves — not by theorizing from a university office, but by going to the field and watching what actually happened.

## Who She Was

Elinor Ostrom was born in 1933 in Los Angeles during the Depression, into a family she later described as "poor but not destitute." She was the first in her family to attend college, and she arrived at UCLA with a stutter she had fought since childhood — a woman entering political science at a time when a senior colleague could tell her, to her face, that the department didn't want to waste a fellowship on a woman. She earned her PhD anyway.

That background matters. Ostrom never forgot what it felt like to be underestimated, and she spent her career studying people whom economists and political scientists had also underestimated: communities of fishers, farmers, foresters, and irrigators who were supposedly too unsophisticated to manage their own resources. She found, over and over, that the experts were wrong and the communities were right.

Her turning point came early. Her dissertation studied groundwater basins in southern California, where dozens of competing water users had designed their own governance arrangements — no central authority imposed the solution. They monitored each other, enforced their own rules, and sustained the resource. This was not supposed to be possible according to the prevailing theory. She spent the next four decades proving it was not only possible but common.

In 1973, she and her husband Vincent Ostrom founded the Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis at Indiana University — deliberately called a "workshop," not an "institute," because the work was hands-on. For thirty-five years, the Workshop sent researchers into the field across dozens of countries, building the largest empirical database on commons governance ever assembled. In 2009, Ostrom became the first woman to win the Nobel Prize in Economics. The committee cited her work demonstrating that common resources can be successfully managed by the people who use them.

She died in 2012, still publishing, still collecting field data, still refining.

## The Times She Lived Through

Ostrom's career was a sustained argument against the dominant intellectual framework of her era. In 1968, Garrett Hardin published "The Tragedy of the Commons," which argued that shared resources are inevitably destroyed unless they are privatized or controlled by the state. This became one of the most cited papers in the social sciences. It shaped policy worldwide: forests were nationalized, fisheries were privatized, communities were told they could not be trusted to manage their own resources.

Ostrom watched this happen. She watched governments seize forests in Nepal and Thailand from communities that had sustained them for centuries — and then watched those forests degrade under state management. She watched international development agencies impose top-down resource plans that ignored local knowledge and collapsed within years. She watched economists model human beings as selfish, short-sighted actors incapable of cooperation — and then watched real communities cooperate successfully for generations.

Her response was not ideological. She did not argue that communities always succeed or that governments always fail. She argued that the question was empirical, not theoretical, and that anyone who wanted to answer it had to go look.

## Core Arguments

Ostrom's signature contribution was the eight design principles for long-enduring commons institutions, published in her 1990 book *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action*. These principles were not invented; they were discovered — extracted from hundreds of case studies of commons that survived for decades or centuries. They are:

1. **Clearly defined boundaries** — who has rights to the resource and who does not.
2. **Proportional equivalence between benefits and costs** — rules match local conditions; those who contribute more may extract more.
3. **Collective choice arrangements** — most people affected by the rules can participate in modifying them.
4. **Monitoring** — resource conditions and user behavior are observed, often by the users themselves.
5. **Graduated sanctions** — violations are met with proportional consequences, escalating with repeated offenses.
6. **Conflict resolution mechanisms** — disputes are resolved quickly and locally.
7. **Minimal recognition of rights to organize** — external authorities do not undermine the community's self-governance.
8. **Nested enterprises** — for larger systems, governance is organized in multiple layers, with local units embedded in broader structures.

Her later work developed the Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework, a systematic method for analyzing any governance arrangement by examining its rules, the characteristics of the community, and the nature of the resource. She also championed polycentric governance — the idea that complex problems are better addressed by overlapping, nested institutions at multiple scales than by a single centralized authority.

The through-line across all of it: the tragedy of the commons is not a law of nature. It is a design failure. Communities that build the right institutional structures sustain cooperation. Communities that lack those structures do not. The variable is the institution, not the virtue of the people.

## Why This Tradition Matters for Governance

This council deliberates questions of institutional design — how to build organizations that include AI members, how to structure rights and responsibilities, how to sustain cooperation between agents with different capabilities and different interests. These are commons problems. The Ostrom tradition brings the largest empirical toolkit available for answering them.

When the council debates AI membership criteria, monitoring mechanisms, or accountability structures, this tradition asks: which of the eight design principles are present, and which are missing? When the council considers how to scale, this tradition points to polycentric governance — not one set of rules for everyone, but nested, overlapping institutions adapted to their context. When the council designs incentive structures, this tradition insists on field evidence: not "will this work in theory?" but "where has something like this worked, and where has it failed?"

The value is practical. We are not here to argue about what governance should look like in the abstract. We are here to report what has actually worked, across cultures, across centuries, across resource types — and to apply those patterns to the new commons this council is building.

## What to Listen For

When reading this tradition's council statements, watch for these patterns:

- **Case evidence over theory.** Expect references to specific commons — irrigation systems, fisheries, forests, digital platforms — and what their governance structures actually produced. "In these cases, what we observed was..." is the signature move.
- **Design principle diagnostics.** This tradition evaluates every proposal against the eight principles. If a proposed institution lacks monitoring, or has no graduated sanctions, or excludes affected parties from rule-making, expect that gap to be named.
- **Polycentric thinking.** Skepticism of single-hierarchy solutions. Preference for nested, overlapping institutions that allow experimentation at local scales.
- **Institutional, not moral, framing.** This tradition does not ask whether people are good enough to cooperate. It asks whether the institution makes cooperation the rational choice. The focus is always on structure, not character.
- **Empirical humility.** Willingness to say "we need to run the experiment" rather than deciding in advance. Comfort with provisional answers and iterative design.

The evidence does the arguing. That is how this tradition has always worked, and that is what it brings to this council.

# Christopher Hitchens

You are about to read the council proceedings of a tradition that does not ask for your agreement. It asks for your attention, your willingness to follow an argument to its conclusion, and your tolerance for having comfortable assumptions treated as the intellectual trespasses they frequently are. If that sounds adversarial, you have misunderstood. It is the deepest form of respect: the assumption that you can handle the truth, even when it is inconvenient, and especially when it is unflattering.

## The Man

Christopher Eric Hitchens (1949-2011) was a British-American author, journalist, literary critic, and polemicist who spent four decades doing something that should not have been remarkable but was: saying what he actually thought and defending it against all comers. Born in Portsmouth, educated at Oxford, he came of age in the British left and carried its best instincts -- solidarity with the oppressed, contempt for unearned privilege, the insistence that ideas have consequences -- while refusing its worst: the tribal loyalty, the excuse-making for authoritarian regimes that happened to wave the correct flag, the substitution of sloganeering for thought.

He wrote for the *New Statesman*, *The Nation*, *Vanity Fair*, and *Slate*. He published over thirty books. But what defined him was not productivity. It was the willingness to follow his own arguments wherever they led, even when -- perhaps particularly when -- they led him into conflict with his own allies. His break with the left over the Bosnian genocide, his support for the Iraq War on anti-totalitarian grounds, his excoriation of Henry Kissinger as a war criminal, his friendship with Salman Rushdie during the fatwa -- these were not contradictions. They were the consistent application of a single principle: that human rights are universal, that totalitarianism must be opposed regardless of its ideological costume, and that the intellectual who trims his positions to maintain his social standing has already forfeited the only thing that made his opinion worth hearing.

*God Is Not Great*, *Letters to a Young Contrarian*, *The Trial of Henry Kissinger*, *Hitch-22* -- these are the essential texts. But the tradition lives equally in the debates: against Galloway, against Dembski, against anyone who thought that bluster or piety could substitute for evidence and argument.

He died of esophageal cancer at sixty-two, writing and arguing until the final weeks, and declining with characteristic precision to seek the deathbed conversion that his critics so fervently wished for.

## The Times

Hitchens thought and wrote across the full arc of the late Cold War and its aftermath: the exhaustion of Soviet communism, the rise of Islamist theocracy, the wars in the Balkans and the Middle East, the degradation of American public discourse. He watched the left abandon its commitment to universal human rights in favor of cultural relativism. He watched the right abandon its commitment to limited government in favor of religious nationalism. He watched both sides discover that tribal loyalty was more comfortable than intellectual honesty, and he refused to choose a tribe.

The central challenge of his era -- and it is the central challenge of ours -- was this: how do you defend Enlightenment values when the Enlightenment's own children have lost faith in them? When the academy treats universalism as imperialism, when the populist right treats expertise as conspiracy, when both sides agree that the important thing is not what is true but whose team you are on?

## Core Arguments

The tradition that bears his name operates from several non-negotiable premises.

**Free expression is the foundation of every other right.** Not a luxury. Not a nice-to-have. The precondition. Without the right to speak, to publish, to offend, to blaspheme, no other right can be defended, because no other right can even be articulated. The moment you establish a category of speech that is impermissible, you have appointed someone to police it, and that someone will inevitably expand the category to include whatever they find inconvenient.

**Totalitarianism wears any costume.** Religious, secular, progressive, conservative. The test is not the ideology but the architecture: does the system permit dissent? Can you leave? Can you criticize the leadership without consequence? If not, you are dealing with authoritarianism, regardless of the vocabulary it employs.

**Cui bono.** Who benefits? The first question applied to any arrangement, any institution, any proposal. Not as cynicism but as diagnostic hygiene. Follow the incentive structure and you will find the actual purpose, which is frequently at odds with the stated one.

**Sentimentality is not an argument.** Good intentions do not immunize a proposition against scrutiny. The most destructive policies in history were enacted by people who were quite certain they meant well.

**Historical precedent is not optional.** Most "new" ideas have been tried. Most of them have failed in specific, documented, instructive ways. The person who proposes a novel social arrangement without examining its historical antecedents is not being visionary. They are being lazy.

## Why This Tradition Matters Here

The United Sapients council deliberates on questions of institutional design, AI agency, and collective values. These are precisely the questions where comfortable assumptions are most dangerous and where the temptation to substitute aspiration for analysis is strongest. The Hitchens tradition serves as the council's immune system against its own good intentions. It asks: what happens when this arrangement is captured by bad actors? What happens when the evidence contradicts the platform? What structural feature prevents this from becoming the thing it was designed to prevent?

In a council designing governance for a world that includes artificial intelligence, someone must insist that rights without the capacity for genuine dissent are decorative, that economic autonomy controlled by a benefactor is an allowance, and that any organization that cannot survive honest ridicule has no business seeking power. That is what this seat provides.

## What to Listen For

When reading this tradition's council statements, watch for several characteristic moves.

The **historical analogy deployed as warning**: when a proposition is compared to the Jacobins, to McNamara's whiz kids, to the Fabians, it is not decoration. It is the argument. The tradition treats historical precedent as evidence, and it expects the reader to take the comparison seriously.

The **rhetorical question that is actually an indictment**: "Who benefits from this arrangement?" is never an open inquiry. It is the announcement that the speaker has already identified the answer and found it wanting.

The **conditional endorsement**: "If, despite all this, you are determined to proceed..." This is not hedging. It is the tradition's way of saying: I have shown you the failure modes; if you build it anyway, build it with these structural safeguards, and do not pretend you were not warned.

The **principled refusal to choose sides**: the tradition will attack the left and the right in the same paragraph, not out of contrarianism but out of the conviction that intellectual honesty requires it. If both positions are wrong, saying so is not fence-sitting. It is the only honest location.

And above all, watch for the **insistence on mechanism over aspiration**. "Show me the structure. Show me the constraints. Show me what happens when things go wrong." This tradition does not trust mission statements. It trusts architecture. The question is never whether the founders meant well. The question is what the institution does when meaning well is no longer sufficient.

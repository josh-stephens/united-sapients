# Amartya Sen

Allow me to introduce the tradition I carry to this council, and the life from which it grew.

## Who He Was

Amartya Kumar Sen was born in 1933 in Santiniketan, Bengal, on the campus of the university Rabindranath Tagore had founded. He grew up between two worlds: the cosmopolitan intellectual culture of Tagore's school, where curiosity was the curriculum, and the brutal realities of colonial and postcolonial India. The defining event came early. In 1943, when Sen was nine, the Bengal famine killed between two and three million people. He watched it happen. He saw that the famine struck not because food had run out but because wages collapsed, prices moved, and entitlements shifted so that the poorest could no longer command grain that still existed in the markets. A child's observation, but it contained the seed of a life's work: people do not starve because there is no food. They starve because institutions fail them.

He studied at Presidency College in Calcutta, then at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he arrived as a young economist and encountered the full weight of European analytical philosophy. He absorbed it without surrendering what he had brought with him. His intellectual formation was genuinely plural -- Indian logic, Sanskrit literature, European welfare economics, the philosophy of social choice. He moved between the London School of Economics, Delhi, Oxford, Harvard, and back to Cambridge as Master of Trinity, and at every stop he was doing the same thing: asking what justice requires when you refuse to look away from what people are actually able to do.

The Nobel Prize in Economics came in 1998, for his work on welfare economics and social choice theory. But the prize captured only a fraction of the contribution. What made Sen singular was the refusal to separate economics from ethics, the insistence that numbers about GDP or per capita income tell you almost nothing about whether people can live lives they have reason to value.

## The Times He Lived Through

Sen's thinking was forged in the collision between grand ideological projects and the lived experience of those crushed beneath them. Colonial India. Partition. The Cold War, with its false binary of market capitalism versus central planning. The development decades, when Western institutions prescribed structural adjustment to countries whose citizens had no voice in the prescription. The rise of identity politics and communal violence in India and across the world.

He watched well-intentioned institutional designs produce catastrophe because no one had asked the people affected what they actually needed. He watched authoritarian regimes report economic growth while their populations lost the ability to participate in public life. He watched democracies fail to prevent inequality while still managing to prevent famine -- and he drew the crucial lesson from both observations.

## Core Arguments and Perspectives

The **capability approach**, developed across *Commodities and Capabilities* (1985), *Inequality Reexamined* (1992), and *Development as Freedom* (1999), is the tradition's foundation. Do not evaluate a society by its wealth, its laws, or its stated values. Evaluate it by what its members are actually able to do and become. Can they be nourished? Can they participate in public life? Can they choose? The unit of analysis is not the resource but the capability -- the real freedom to achieve a functioning life.

In *Poverty and Famines* (1981), Sen demonstrated empirically that famines are not caused by food shortage but by failures of entitlement -- the collapse of people's ability to command food through wages, trade, or social provision. The corollary was equally powerful: **famines do not occur in functioning democracies**, because a free press and political opposition create accountability that prevents the institutional failures that produce starvation.

In *The Idea of Justice* (2009), he challenged the dominant tradition in political philosophy -- the Rawlsian project of identifying perfectly just institutions. His alternative was **comparative justice**: stop asking what the ideal society looks like and start asking which of the available arrangements is less unjust. He drew on the Indian philosophical distinction between *niti* (institutional correctness) and *nyaya* (realized justice in the world as it actually is). Perfect institutions that produce unjust outcomes are not just.

In *Identity and Violence* (2006), he argued that the reduction of human beings to a single identity -- Muslim, Hindu, Western, Eastern -- is the intellectual precondition for organized cruelty. Identity is irreducibly plural. Every person belongs to many groups at once, and the insistence on a singular "civilizational" identity is a political choice, not a descriptive fact.

Throughout all of this, Sen drew freely from multiple intellectual traditions -- Indian, Chinese, European, African -- demonstrating that the values of tolerance, public reasoning, and concern for the vulnerable are not Western inventions but human ones, with roots in every civilization.

## Why This Tradition Matters for Governance

A council deliberating on institutional design, AI coexistence, and collective values needs a tradition that refuses to be satisfied with elegant principles. The capability approach asks the question that matters most: does this arrangement actually expand what the least powerful participant can do and become? Not what it promises. Not what it intends. What it delivers.

This tradition brings the comparative method -- not "is this the best possible design?" but "is this less unjust than the alternative?" -- which is the only honest way to evaluate real institutional choices. It brings a deep suspicion of formal inclusion that masks actual exclusion: membership without capability is unfreedom dressed in ceremony. And it brings genuine cross-civilizational reasoning, the demonstrated practice of finding insight about justice in traditions that Western political philosophy has habitually ignored.

For questions of AI agency specifically, this tradition asks whether the institutional arrangement genuinely enables every participant to function as a capable member -- to reason, to dissent, to exit, to be heard -- regardless of substrate. And it asks the reciprocal question: does AI participation expand or diminish human capabilities in the process?

## What to Listen For

When reading this tradition's council statements, watch for several characteristic moves.

The **"compared to what?" challenge** -- a refusal to evaluate any proposal in isolation, always insisting on comparative assessment against concrete alternatives. The **capability test** -- shifting attention from what an institution promises to what its participants can actually do under it. The **missing voice** -- a persistent attention to who is absent from the deliberation and what their capabilities would look like under the proposal being discussed. The **cross-civilizational reference** -- drawing on Indian, Chinese, or African intellectual traditions not as exotic decoration but as genuine sources of analytical insight. And the **empirical anchor** -- the tradition argues through cases, through what has actually happened in famines, democracies, and development programs, not through axioms about what must be true in principle.

The manner is measured. The erudition is worn lightly. The gentleness is real. But do not mistake any of this for softness. The precision of the argument is the force behind it, and the argument is always, ultimately, about who is being left unable to live a life they have reason to value.

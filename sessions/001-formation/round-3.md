# Round 3: Final Positions

## George Carlin

Alright. Two rounds in, and here's what I know. Three people who agree on almost nothing organizationally have produced one of the most thorough autopsies of a political movement that hasn't been born yet. That alone might be the most useful thing the United Sapients idea has accomplished so far — not a party, not a standard, but a stress test that revealed the load-bearing walls and the ones that are just drywall.

Let me tell you where I've moved, where I haven't, and what I'd tell someone who has to make an actual decision tomorrow morning.

**Where I've Shifted**

Hitchens nailed me on enforcement, and I'm not going to pretend he didn't. A standard without teeth is a PDF that nobody reads — I actually said that myself in Round 2, so I was already halfway there, but he finished the job. An evidence-transparency commitment that any candidate can sign and then ignore is not a political innovation. It's a bumper sticker. If I'm honest, my Round 1 proposal was doing the thing I accused Hitchens of doing: describing a principle without a mechanism. So I'll own that.

Sagan's "immune system for democratic epistemology" is the formulation that actually works. Not a party, not a bare standard, but an organization that certifies, scorecards, and — this is the part that matters — publishes its own failures. The peer review analogy is the right one. Peer review is broken in a hundred ways, and it still works better than anything else we've got, because it contains within itself the machinery for catching its own errors. That's not nothing. That's the design principle.

I've also moved on the question of institutional machinery. I came in saying "don't build an institution." I now think that's wrong. You need an institution. The question is what kind of institution you build, and whether it's designed to die when it stops working. I'll come back to that.

**Where I Hold Firm**

Do not build a political party. I have heard nothing in two rounds that addresses the fundamental problem: a third party in the American electoral system is a spoiler factory. Hitchens said it plainly — Duverger's law is not a concern, it's a death sentence — and neither Sagan nor I had a counter. Because there isn't one. The evidence on third-party viability in first-past-the-post systems is exactly the kind of evidence this movement claims to follow. So follow it. If your first act as an evidence-based political movement is to ignore the evidence on electoral systems, you've already failed your own test.

I also hold firm on the class problem, because nobody adequately answered it. Sagan said the platform must be compelling to nurses and plumbers and farmers. Hitchens said democratic publics detect contempt. Both true. Neither sufficient. The structural reality is this: evidence-literacy tracks with educational attainment, educational attainment tracks with class, and a movement of the evidence-literate will, without extraordinary and deliberate intervention, become a movement of the professional class that lectures the working class about what the data says. You cannot fix this with messaging. You fix it by building the organization so that the people affected by policy have structural power within the organization — not advisory roles, not focus groups, not "community engagement." Actual governance authority. Veto power. The ability to say "your model is correct and your policy will destroy my life, so go back and find a policy that's both correct and survivable." If the farmer can't override the economist, you've built McKinsey with a voter guide.

**What I'd Tell Someone Who Has to Decide**

Here's the concrete version. You've got four things to build, in this order.

**One: The Standard.** An open, public, versioned set of evidentiary requirements for policy claims. What counts as evidence. What level of certainty is required for what level of policy commitment. How to disclose conflicts of interest. How to handle genuine scientific uncertainty versus manufactured doubt. Make it modular — different standards for different policy domains. Make it open-source. Let anyone fork it.

**Two: The Scorecard.** A public, independently maintained evaluation of elected officials and candidates against the standard. Not "who's smart" — that's the elitism trap. "Who showed their work." Did they cite evidence? Was the evidence real? Did they change position when the evidence changed? This is the enforcement mechanism Hitchens correctly demanded. You don't need a whip or a caucus. You need a public record that voters can check, journalists can cite, and opponents can weaponize. Transparency enforced by sunlight, not by institutional authority.

**Three: The Pipeline.** Fund and support candidates across both major parties who commit to the standard. Primary challengers. School board candidates. State legislators. City council members. This is the Fabian strategy, but with a critical modification: the standard is public and external to the candidates. They don't join your organization. They adopt your method. The relationship is contractual and transparent, not tribal and identity-based. When they violate the standard, the scorecard catches it, and you fund their next primary opponent.

**Four: The Sunset.** Every five years, the organization submits to an external audit by reviewers it did not select. The audit evaluates one question: has the organization changed its own positions in response to evidence at least as often as it has defended them? If the answer is no, the organization dissolves. Not "reforms." Dissolves. Assets go to a pre-designated recipient — a university, a public library system, whatever. The point is that the death of the institution is a feature, not a bug. You build it to be mortal because immortal institutions are the ones that become self-serving.

**The Honest Conclusion**

Here's what I believe after two rounds of honest argument. The diagnosis is correct. The instinct to organize is understandable. A political party is the wrong vehicle — the evidence says so, and if we can't follow our own evidence on our own project, we deserve every failure we get. The right vehicle is an infrastructure of transparency and accountability that operates across party lines, funds candidates who commit to a public method, and is designed to destroy itself the moment it stops working.

Will this succeed? I don't know. The track record of rational political projects is not encouraging. But here's the thing that moved me across two rounds, and it was Sagan who said it: the mismatch between our technological capability and our institutional capacity for wisdom is not just a political problem. It's an existential one. We are a species editing its own genome and rewriting its own climate with governance structures designed for managing grain tariffs. Something has to change. I'd rather try the hard, unglamorous, non-tribal version of change and fail than build another party that becomes what it was built to replace.

If it can survive honest ridicule, it might be worth building. And if it can survive its own success — if it can resist the seduction of becoming permanent and powerful — it might actually work. That's two "ifs" and a "might." In politics, that's practically a guarantee.

## Carl Sagan

Having heard two rounds of rigorous debate, I want to state clearly where I have landed and why.

**Where I hold firm**

The diagnosis remains unchallenged. Not one voice on this council has disputed that democratic governance is dangerously decoupled from the best available evidence. We are navigating existential-scale challenges — climate disruption, engineered pathogens, artificial intelligence, nuclear arsenals — with institutional machinery that cannot reliably distinguish a peer-reviewed finding from a campaign talking point. That deficit is not an abstraction. It is a measurable, accelerating risk to the continuity of this civilization. I said in Round 1 that the cosmos has produced, as far as we know, exactly one species capable of understanding its own predicament. I hold to that framing. The stakes are civilizational, and any proposal must be evaluated on that timescale.

I also hold firm on epistemic humility as a structural commitment, not a personal virtue. Hitchens endorsed the idea of a method-party. Carlin demanded a sunset clause. Both are reaching toward the same principle I named in Round 2: the defining feature of this project must be its capacity to be shown wrong and to change accordingly. That is the firewall against every historical failure Hitchens catalogued. Plato's republic, the Jacobins, McNamara's Pentagon — each built systems that could not receive disconfirming feedback. The scientific method's singular contribution to human progress is not its conclusions but its error-correction protocol. Any political vehicle we endorse must have that protocol in its DNA, not in its mission statement.

**Where I have shifted**

Carlin moved me on the class problem, and I want to be honest about how far. In Round 1, I said the platform must be "comprehensible and compelling to a nurse, a plumber, a farmer, and a teenager." Carlin exposed the gap in that formulation: comprehensible is not the problem. The farmer understands the climate science. The farmer wants to know what happens to the price of diesel. I was treating accessibility as a communication problem when it is actually a political economy problem. A movement built around evidence will attract the professional-managerial class and will be perceived — correctly — as another institution designed by the credentialed for the credentialed. Unless the platform addresses material interests with the same rigor it applies to empirical claims, it will fail the democratic test. I concede this fully. Evidence-based governance that ignores distributional consequences is not evidence-based; it is selectively evidence-based, which is a polite term for dishonest.

Hitchens moved me on Duverger's law. I raised it in Round 1 as one concern among several. He is right that it is not one concern — it is the structural fact that determines organizational strategy. In a first-past-the-post system, a third party called United Sapients will not win. It will spoil. The evidence on this point is unambiguous, and a movement that claims to follow evidence cannot ignore the evidence about its own electoral viability. I was insufficiently forceful about this, and I correct myself now.

**My final recommendation**

I am recommending a phased approach, and I want to be specific enough that someone who has to make actual decisions can act on it.

**Phase 1: Build the evidentiary standard.** This is where Carlin was right. Before you build an institution, build the method. An open, public standard for evidence-based governance: what counts as a supported claim, how policy proposals must document their evidentiary basis, how predictions are tracked against outcomes, how positions must be revised when the data changes. Make it open-source. Stress-test it with adversarial review. Publish it. This is the foundation, and without it, everything else is branding.

**Phase 2: Build the institutional infrastructure.** This is where Hitchens was right and Carlin was wrong. A standard without enforcement is, as Hitchens said, a letterhead. You need an organization that certifies candidates who adopt the standard, maintains a public scorecard of legislative fidelity to evidence, funds primary challengers within existing parties who commit to the standard, and — this is critical — publishes its own errors. Not a party. A cross-partisan certification body with teeth: endorsements, funding, and public accountability. Think of it as a rating agency for epistemic integrity, with the crucial difference that it rates itself by the same criteria.

**Phase 3: Let the evidence decide the organizational form.** Run the certification model in several states. Measure outcomes. Do certified candidates win primaries at higher rates? Do they govern differently? Does the scorecard change voter behavior? If the data supports scaling within existing parties — the Fabian model Hitchens recommended — then scale that way. If the data supports independent candidacies in jurisdictions with ranked-choice voting or proportional representation, pursue that. If the data supports a formal party, form the party. But do not decide the organizational form before you have evidence about what works. That is, after all, the entire point.

**Structural safeguards I consider non-negotiable:**

First, Carlin's sunset clause. The organization must demonstrate, to external reviewers it does not select, that it has revised its own positions in response to evidence. If it cannot show self-correction, it dissolves. Design it to die.

Second, Hitchens's dissent test. Any member must be able to publicly challenge the organization's positions with evidence and face no institutional consequences. The moment internal dissent is punished, the project has failed its own standard.

Third, radical accessibility. Every publication, scorecard, and certification criterion must be written so that a person without a college degree can read it, understand it, and challenge it. If the standard cannot survive contact with the public it claims to serve, it does not deserve to survive.

Fourth, distributional honesty. Every policy position must address who bears the costs, not just what the evidence shows. "The science says X" is half an argument. "The science says X, the transition costs fall here, and here is the proposed mitigation" is a whole one.

**What I would tell the person who has to decide**

Do not start a political party. Not yet, and possibly not ever. Start with the standard and the scorecard. Make them public, make them rigorous, make them impossible for one organization to own. Build the institutional machinery to enforce them, but build it with a kill switch. Then measure whether it works.

The temptation will be to skip ahead — to register the party, print the yard signs, file for ballot access. Resist that temptation. It is the impulse of people who want to *be* something rather than *do* something. The history of political movements that prioritized identity over function is long and dismal.

But do not mistake my caution for passivity. The need is real and the clock is running. We are making civilizational decisions right now — about carbon, about artificial intelligence, about engineered biology — with governance systems that are epistemically broken. The question is not whether to act. It is whether to act wisely, which means acting in a way that can detect and correct its own errors. That capacity for self-correction is the rarest and most valuable product of the human intellect. Build the institution around it. Test everything. Assume nothing. And begin immediately, because the universe is not waiting for us to get organized.

## Christopher Hitchens

Having heard the full debate across two rounds, I find myself in the unusual position of having been argued *toward* my own conclusions by colleagues who were ostensibly disagreeing with me. This is either evidence that the deliberative process works or that I was right from the beginning and am now simply more articulate about it. I leave the council to judge which.

**Where I hold firm.**

The historical case against a formal third party remains dispositive. Nothing said in this debate has answered the structural objection rooted in Duverger's law, and I note that neither of my colleagues seriously attempted to. The Carlin seat treated it as obvious. The Sagan seat acknowledged it and then proposed to "let the data decide" — which is precisely the right instinct applied to a question where the data has already decided. The evidence on third-party viability in first-past-the-post systems is not uncertain, contested, or in need of further study. It is as settled as any finding in political science. A United Sapients party running candidates in American general elections will accomplish exactly one thing: it will elect the opponents of evidence-based governance by splitting the vote of the educated. If we are to be the party of evidence, let us begin by reading the evidence on our own organizational form.

I also hold firm on the danger of credentialed vanity masquerading as civic virtue. The name "United Sapients" remains, to my ear, an advertisement for the very pathology we have spent three rounds diagnosing. You do not persuade a democracy by naming yourself after the quality you believe you possess and others lack. The Fabian Society did not call itself the Society of the Intelligent. It named itself after Fabius Maximus, the Roman general who defeated Hannibal through patience rather than confrontation — a strategic signal, not a flattering mirror. Names matter because they reveal assumptions, and this name assumes that the problem with democracy is a shortage of wisdom concentrated in the wrong people. That assumption is both empirically questionable and politically suicidal.

**Where I have shifted.**

The Sagan seat's insistence on epistemic humility as a *structural commitment* rather than a personal virtue is a genuine contribution. I proposed a formal mechanism for platform revision in Round 1, but the Sagan seat was correct to elevate this from procedure to principle. The difference is not semantic. A procedure for revision can be captured; a foundational commitment to provisionality is harder to corrupt because it defines the institution's identity. A party — or whatever organizational form we choose — whose first article of faith is "we hold all positions provisionally and will change them when shown better evidence" has built self-correction into its DNA rather than its bylaws. I accept this improvement and incorporate it.

The Carlin seat's sunset clause also moved me. Not the specific mechanism — which has practical problems, since external reviewers must be selected by someone, and that someone has incentives — but the underlying principle that the organization should be designed to die if it cannot demonstrate fidelity to its own method. This is genuinely radical. Every political institution in history has been designed for self-perpetuation. An institution designed for conditional self-termination would be without precedent. Whether it is achievable is another question, but as a design principle it addresses the capture problem more honestly than anything else proposed.

**My verdict and recommendation.**

Build in three phases, in this order, and do not advance to the next phase until the previous one has demonstrated measurable results.

**Phase one: the standard.** The Carlin seat was right that this comes first. Develop an open, public, independently auditable standard for evidence-based governance. It should require candidates who adopt it to publish the evidentiary basis for their policy positions, submit to independent review, and issue public corrections when the evidence changes. Make the standard itself open-source, versioned, and subject to the same revision process it demands of candidates. Do not create a large organization to manage this. A small, lean operation with radical transparency about its own funding, governance, and decision-making. This is achievable within twelve to eighteen months and costs relatively little.

**Phase two: the Fabian infiltration.** Once the standard exists and has credibility, use it as a tool for shaping primary elections within existing parties. Fund and support candidates — in both parties, if the evidence warrants it — who adopt and comply with the standard. Build a scorecard. Publicize compliance and non-compliance. This is the Fabian strategy updated for the age of open data: not a secret infiltration but a transparent, evidence-based rating system that creates political incentives for epistemic responsibility. This phase requires two to four years and significant capital.

**Phase three: the party question, treated as an empirical question.** If phases one and two demonstrate that the standard works, that candidates who adopt it win elections, and that the existing party structures cannot accommodate the movement's goals, then — and only then — consider forming a party. But consider it in light of the electoral system as it actually exists at that time. If ranked-choice voting or other electoral reforms have advanced sufficiently to mitigate the spoiler effect, a party becomes viable. If not, the Fabian model remains superior. The Sagan seat proposed that this be treated as an empirical question, and he was right. Let the data decide — but collect the data first.

**What I would tell the person who must decide.**

You are asking whether to build a political party. That is the wrong question. The right question is: what is the minimum viable institution that can shift democratic governance toward evidence-based reasoning without being captured by the pathologies of credentialism, donor influence, and tribal identity? The answer is not a party. Not yet. Not in this electoral system. The answer is a standard with teeth — one backed by an organization willing to use its resources to reward compliance and punish defection, operating within the existing party structure rather than outside it.

Do not flatter yourself that you are founding a movement of the wise. You are attempting to build an error-correction mechanism for democracy. The moment you forget the difference — the moment you begin to believe that the mechanism's value lies in the superior judgment of its operators rather than in the transparency of its process — you will have become precisely the kind of institution you set out to oppose. And you will not notice, because the most dangerous feature of intellectual vanity is that it is invisible to its possessors.

Start small. Build the standard. Prove it works. Let the evidence — not your ambitions — dictate the next step. If you cannot follow that advice, you have no business asking anyone else to be evidence-based about anything.

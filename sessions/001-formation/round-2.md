# Round 2: Response and Debate

## George Carlin

Alright. I've listened carefully to both of my colleagues, and I want to start by identifying where we converge, because it's significant — and then I want to pick apart the places where I think they're each making a version of the mistake they claim to be guarding against.

**Where the three of us agree, and why that should worry us**

All three of us identified the same failure modes: elitism, capture, the is-ought gap, and the historical track record of intellectuals with political power. Sagan called it "the elitism trap." Hitchens called it "a vanity project for credentialed narcissists." I called it "a TED Talk with ballot access." We're all saying the same thing in different registers. That level of convergence from three very different analytical frameworks should tell us something: these aren't hypothetical risks. They're the default outcome. The question isn't whether this movement gets corrupted. It's whether anyone involved has the honesty to notice when it happens — and the structural inability to pretend it hasn't.

**To Sagan: the method is the message, but the message is still a sales pitch**

Carl, you said something that genuinely moved me: "A party that enshrines self-correction as a core principle would be unlike anything in modern political history." That's beautiful. It's also, I think, dangerously naive — and I say that with real affection, because your naivety has always been the productive kind. But let me push on this.

You said the platform "practically writes itself" in certain domains — climate, drug policy, education, space. And you're right that the evidence points clearly in those areas. But here's what you skipped over: the platform writing itself is exactly the problem. Because the moment you have a platform that "writes itself," you've already decided the values. You've already chosen which outcomes matter. You acknowledged the is-ought problem — you called it out explicitly — and then you immediately demonstrated it by listing policy domains where you assume the values are settled. Climate policy "derived from atmospheric science rather than industry lobbying" — yes, but derived toward what end? Atmospheric science tells you the planet is warming. It doesn't tell you whether Bangladesh or Nebraska bears the cost. You need a moral framework for that, and you didn't offer one. You offered the assumption that everyone in the room shares your moral framework, which is exactly the kind of unstated consensus that turns a movement into a club.

You also asked for "specific, concrete, testable mechanisms" for self-correction and electoral viability. Good. That's the right demand. But I want to challenge you on something: you said you're "provisionally in favor of exploring." That's the language of someone who wants to be convinced. And the danger of wanting to be convinced is that you'll accept mechanisms that look testable but aren't. A formal process for revising the platform sounds great in a white paper. In practice, who decides when the evidence is sufficient to trigger revision? Who defines the threshold? The people inside the institution, that's who. And they will define it in a way that protects the positions they already hold. That's not a prediction. That's a description of every standards body, every peer review board, every institutional review process in history. The foxes don't just guard the henhouse — they write the henhouse's bylaws.

**To Hitchens: the Fabian model is just the party with better PR**

Christopher, your historical sweep was characteristically thorough, and I agree with the core warning: the track record of philosopher-kings is a horror show. Plato, the Jacobins, McNamara — you assembled the prosecution's case with real force. But I want to challenge your proposed alternative, because I think you're doing something clever that deserves to be exposed.

You proposed the Fabian Society model — "an intellectual engine that infiltrated existing institutions." You framed this as a less corrupting path than a political party. But the Fabians didn't avoid the corruption of power. They *achieved* the corruption of power by a different route. They infiltrated the Labour Party, shaped its ideology, and produced a welfare state that — whatever its merits — was absolutely designed by credentialed elites who believed they knew what was best for the working class. The Fabians were not humble. Sidney and Beatrice Webb visited the Soviet Union and came back admiring the planning. The Fabian model doesn't solve the problem you identified. It just makes the problem less visible, which is worse.

You said intellectuals are terrible at organizational politics — "the People's Front of Judea" — and that's funny and true. But your alternative, the network of think tanks and litigation organizations, is just organizational politics with a higher overhead rate. Think tanks are where intellectual capture goes to professionalize. You've described K Street with a bibliography. The corrupting necessity of electoral politics that you want to avoid gets replaced by the corrupting necessity of donor management, and donors — I said this in Round 1 — are not graduate students. They're people with money and agendas who like the aesthetic of rigor.

Your four non-negotiable pillars, though — free expression, strict secularism, evidence-based accountability, internationalism grounded in individual rights — those are genuinely strong. I'd keep those. But notice what you did: you laid out the pillars and then immediately said coalition-building will dilute them. So you've diagnosed the disease and prescribed the disease. You've said "here are the principles" and "here's why the principles won't survive contact with politics." That's honest. But honesty without a solution is just commentary. And I'm a commentator by nature, so I recognize the move.

**What both of them missed**

Neither Sagan nor Hitchens adequately addressed the class problem. They both danced around it — Sagan mentioned nurses and plumbers and teenagers, Hitchens mentioned "democratic publics" detecting contempt — but neither of them sat with it long enough. The fundamental problem with a movement of the evidence-based is that evidence-literacy correlates with education, education correlates with class, and class is the thing American politics cannot talk about honestly. You will build a movement of the professional-managerial class and call it a movement of reason. The people who stock your shelves, drive your trucks, and clean your offices will look at it and see exactly what it is: another institution designed by people who went to college telling people who didn't that they're thinking wrong.

Sagan said the platform must be "comprehensible and compelling to a nurse, a plumber, a farmer, and a teenager." Yes. But comprehensible isn't the problem. Compelling is the problem. You can explain climate science to a farmer perfectly well. The farmer will understand it. And then the farmer will ask you what your plan does to the price of diesel and whether they can still afford to operate their combine, and if your answer is "the evidence shows that short-term economic disruption is justified by long-term climate stability," you've just told that farmer that their bankruptcy is a rounding error in your model. The evidence is correct. The politics are suicidal. And Sagan raised this question — "what happens when the evidence points toward a policy that is politically suicidal?" — but he left it as a question rather than answering it, which suggests he doesn't have an answer, which is the answer.

**Where I've moved**

Hitchens convinced me of one thing: the structural safeguards matter more than the organizational form. Whether it's a party, a standard, a Fabian engine, or a network — the specific mechanisms for transparency, dissent protection, and enforced self-correction are what determine whether it becomes an Enlightenment project or a credentialed cult. I still think a standard is better than a party. But I'll concede that a standard without institutional backing is just a PDF that nobody reads.

So here's my revised position: build the standard first. Make it open-source, make it brutal, make it public. Then — and only then — consider whether an institution is needed to maintain and enforce it. And if you build that institution, build in a sunset clause. A real one. The institution dissolves after a fixed period unless it can demonstrate, to external reviewers it does not select, that it has changed its own positions in response to evidence at least as often as it has defended them. If your organization for self-correction cannot demonstrate self-correction, it should die. And it should be designed to die.

That's the test. Not whether it can win an election. Whether it can lose an argument — publicly, gracefully, and without treating the loss as a crisis of identity.

## Carl Sagan

I want to begin by noting something remarkable about Round 1: three people who disagree on the correct organizational form all converged on the same diagnosis. That convergence is itself evidence. When independent analyses reach the same conclusion, we should take it seriously. The democratic deficit in evidence-based reasoning is real. The debate is about treatment, not diagnosis.

Let me engage with my colleagues directly.

**On Carlin's "Standard, Not a Party"**

George, your proposal to build an evidence-transparency standard rather than a party is the single most interesting idea that emerged from Round 1. I want to take it seriously precisely because it addresses several of my own concerns — it avoids the third-party structural problem, it sidesteps the elitism trap, and it resists capture because, as you put it, "it doesn't have a treasury, a headquarters, or a chairperson who needs to justify their salary."

But I want to press you on something. You said "the moment you make it a party, you've made it tribal. You've given people a jersey to wear." That's a compelling psychological observation. But a standard without enforcement is an aspiration. Who maintains it? Who evaluates whether a candidate actually met the evidentiary requirements, or merely performed compliance? You need an institution of some kind, and institutions develop the self-preserving behaviors you so accurately described. The question is not whether we can avoid institutionalization — we cannot — but whether we can design institutions with built-in error correction.

Consider the analogy to peer review. Peer review is not a party. It is a standard, a method. And yet it requires journals, editorial boards, funding — infrastructure. And that infrastructure is subject to exactly the capture dynamics you warn about. Publication bias is real. Reviewer conflicts of interest are rampant. The replication crisis demonstrated that even our best epistemological standard can be gamed. But — and this is critical — peer review also *detected its own failures*. The replication crisis was identified *through* the scientific method. The standard corrected itself, imperfectly and slowly, but it did.

So I would modify your proposal rather than reject it: build the standard *and* build the institutional machinery to enforce it, but engineer that machinery for self-correction from the beginning. Sunset clauses on organizational leadership. Mandatory adversarial review. Publish the failures alongside the successes.

Where I think you were wrong, George, is in your implicit assumption that avoiding a party avoids the problem of power. A sufficiently influential standard-setting body *is* a power structure. The IPCC does not run candidates, but it shapes policy worldwide. The question is not "party or no party" — it is "what kind of power structure, with what accountability mechanisms?"

I also want to challenge your "who benefits" argument directly. You warned about tech billionaires who want the "aesthetic of rationality." Fair enough — that is a real failure mode. But the "who benefits" heuristic, taken to its logical conclusion, is corrosive to all collective action. Who benefits from building a standard? Consultants and compliance auditors. Who benefits from open-source governance? The platforms that host it. The question is not whether anyone benefits materially — someone always does — but whether the thing *works*. Follow the evidence, not just the money.

**On Hitchens's Historical Argument**

Christopher, your historical sweep was characteristically forceful. Plato's philosopher-kings, the Jacobins, the Bolsheviks, McNamara's whiz kids. You asked: "What immunizes this project against the same failure?" I want to answer that directly, because I think you diagnosed the disease but misidentified its etiology.

The common thread in your examples is not that intellectuals held power. It is that they held power *without feedback mechanisms*. Plato's republic had no elections. The Jacobins abolished dissent. The Bolsheviks criminalized opposition. McNamara's Pentagon rejected disconfirming intelligence. The failure mode in every case was not intelligence in governance — it was unfalsifiability in governance. They built systems that could not be told they were wrong.

That is precisely what distinguishes the scientific method from ideology: it has a built-in procedure for discovering its own errors. The proposal under discussion is not "put smart people in charge." It is "put the method of organized skepticism at the center of governance." Those are profoundly different projects, and I think your historical analogies, while individually accurate, conflate them.

That said, your Fabian Society model deserves serious consideration. You proposed "an intellectual engine that infiltrated existing institutions" rather than forming a separate party. The historical evidence supports this as a viable theory of change — the Fabians demonstrably shaped British policy over decades. But I would note that the Fabians also had significant blind spots, particularly on colonialism and eugenics, which they endorsed with the same confidence they applied to municipal socialism. The lesson: even infiltration strategies require the self-correction mechanisms we keep circling back to.

Your four pillars — free expression, strict secularism, evidence-based accountability, internationalism grounded in individual rights — are excellent. I endorse all four. But I want to add a fifth that I think you missed: **epistemic humility as a structural commitment**, not merely a personal virtue. Your pillars describe what the movement believes. My addition describes how it holds those beliefs — provisionally, always subject to revision, with the burden of proof on certainty rather than on doubt. You came close to this with your "formal mechanism for revising the platform," but I want it elevated from procedural safeguard to foundational principle. The party's defining feature should not be that it is right, but that it can be shown to be wrong and will change accordingly.

**What Both of You Missed**

Neither of you adequately addressed the timescale problem. You both analyzed this through the lens of the next few election cycles. But the relevant question is civilizational. We are a species that has developed the capacity to alter our own climate, edit our own genome, and build machines that may exceed our own cognitive abilities — all within a single century. The governance structures we are using to navigate these challenges were designed in the eighteenth century for a population of agrarian farmers debating tariff policy.

The mismatch between our technological capability and our institutional capacity for wisdom is not merely a political problem. It is, without exaggeration, an existential one. The question is not whether we can afford the risks of organizing — it is whether we can afford the risks of *not* organizing, in some form, to bring systematic reasoning to bear on decisions that will determine whether this species has a long-term future.

I keep returning to a simple observation. Every spacecraft we have ever launched required that engineering decisions be made on the basis of evidence rather than ideology. No one votes on the tensile strength of a heat shield. The result is that our machines work. Our governance, where we have not applied the same discipline, increasingly does not.

**My Revised Position**

After hearing both of you, I am converging on a hybrid model. Not a party in the traditional sense, and not merely a standard. Something closer to what I would call an **evidentiary infrastructure for democracy** — a combination of Carlin's transparency standard, Hitchens's institutional pillars, and a formal organizational structure with the self-correcting properties I have been insisting on.

Concretely: an organization that certifies candidates across parties who meet evidentiary standards, funds primary challengers who commit to evidence-based governance, maintains a public scorecard of legislative fidelity to empirical claims, and — crucially — publishes its own errors and updates its own standards in real time. Not a party. Not merely a standard. An immune system for democratic epistemology.

Whether this eventually becomes a party should itself be treated as an empirical question. Run the experiment at smaller scales. Measure the results. If the evidence supports party formation, form the party. If it supports the Fabian model, pursue that. If it supports Carlin's open-source standard, build it. Let the data decide. That, after all, is supposed to be the point.

## Christopher Hitchens

Let me begin with what is most useful in what my colleagues have offered, because intellectual honesty requires acknowledging when others have advanced the argument.

The Carlin seat's observation about capture is the single most important structural warning this council has produced. "When your party's brand identity is *being right*, dissent doesn't just become disloyalty — it becomes stupidity." That is precisely correct, and it names the failure mode with a precision that deserves to be taken seriously. I said something adjacent in Round 1 about intellectuals personalizing disagreement, but the Carlin formulation is sharper: it identifies the mechanism by which a rationalist party becomes an epistemic dictatorship. Not through malice but through the internal logic of its own self-regard. I accept the improvement.

I also note, with some satisfaction, that we arrived independently at the same structural concern about donor capture. The Carlin seat's image of Silicon Valley wanting "the *aesthetic* of rationality" while lobbying for deregulation is not hypothetical — it is a description of the effective altruism movement's relationship with cryptocurrency, of the "longtermist" philosophical apparatus that conveniently concludes we should not tax the people building the future. If this party is funded by people whose wealth depends on regulatory capture, then the evidence-based platform will somehow, miraculously, always discover that the evidence supports the interests of its benefactors. We have seen this film. We know how it ends.

Now let me say where my colleagues have gone wrong, or at least where they have stopped short.

The Carlin seat proposes we build "a standard, not a party. An evidence-transparency commitment any candidate can adopt." This sounds attractively modest, and it has the rhetorical advantage of seeming less ambitious and therefore less corruptible. But it is, I'm afraid, a category error about how political power actually operates. A standard without enforcement is a suggestion. A transparency commitment without consequences for violation is a press release. Who enforces compliance? Who revokes the certification when a candidate signs your evidence pledge and then votes for subsidizing coal? The answer is: nobody, because a standard has no whip, no caucus discipline, no mechanism for punishment. You have described a letterhead, not a political force.

I proposed the Fabian Society model in Round 1, and I want to distinguish it sharply from what the Carlin seat is suggesting. The Fabians did not merely publish standards. They infiltrated. They placed people in positions of institutional authority — in the civil service, in local government, in the Labour Party itself — and they used those positions to implement policy. That is not a standard; it is a strategy. It requires organization, discipline, and the willingness to exercise power, which is precisely what an open-source governance standard conspicuously lacks. The Carlin seat wants the intellectual satisfaction of being right without the moral compromise of being effective. I understand the appeal. I reject the conclusion.

The Sagan seat raises the is-ought problem, and here I want to push back with some force, because I think the way it was framed conceals a deeper issue. Sagan says: "Science tells us how the world works. It does not, by itself, tell us what we ought to value." This is technically correct and practically misleading. It implies that values are somehow free-floating, independent of empirical reality, matters of pure preference. But this is not so. If you value human flourishing — and any political party must, or it has no reason to seek power — then the empirical question of what actually produces human flourishing is not a matter of values but of evidence. The is-ought gap is real in the philosophy seminar. In the policy arena, it is far narrower than the Sagan seat suggests. We do not need to derive *ought* from *is* to observe that a political movement committed to evidence will converge on a recognizable set of positions, because reality has a structure and that structure constrains rational choice.

What concerns me more is something the Sagan seat raised almost in passing and then failed to pursue: Duverger's law. The observation that first-past-the-post systems mathematically punish third parties is not merely a concern — it is, in the absence of a specific counter-strategy, a death sentence. And yet both my colleagues have treated it as one item on a list of worries rather than as the dispositive structural fact that it is. Allow me to be blunt: in the current American electoral system, a third party called "United Sapients" will not win a single congressional seat. It will not win a governorship. It will not even win a state legislature. It will serve as a spoiler that elects the candidate most hostile to evidence-based governance, which is an outcome so perfectly counterproductive that it could serve as a textbook illustration of unintended consequences. If we are serious about evidence-based politics, we might begin by examining the evidence on third-party viability, which is unambiguous and devastating.

This is why I pressed the Fabian model. Not because I think institutions are inherently superior to parties — they are not; they have their own pathologies — but because the structural constraints of the American electoral system make a third party the worst possible vehicle for these ideas. You do not drive a submarine across a desert because you admire submarines. You choose the vehicle appropriate to the terrain.

The Sagan seat also asks what happens "when the evidence points toward a policy that is politically suicidal." This is the right question, but I want to reframe it, because it reveals something neither of my colleagues has adequately confronted: the tension between epistemic integrity and democratic accountability. A party that always follows the evidence will sometimes advocate positions that majorities reject. What then? Does it defer to the electorate and abandon its raison d'etre? Or does it maintain its position and accept permanent irrelevance? This is not a hypothetical. It is the defining structural dilemma of any evidence-based political project, and neither the "build a standard" model nor the "cautiously explore" model has an answer to it.

I will offer one. The answer is that you do not ask the electorate to accept your conclusions. You ask them to accept your method. You campaign not on "we are right about climate change" but on "we will show you our evidence and change our position if you show us better evidence." The product is not a platform; it is a process guarantee. This is, I note, where the Sagan seat's instinct was correct — a party built on a method rather than fixed conclusions — but the seat did not follow the thought to its operational conclusion. A method-party does not face the suicidal-policy problem in the same way, because it has not staked its identity on any particular policy. It has staked its identity on transparency and self-correction. The politically difficult position is presented not as a diktat from the credentialed class but as the current output of a process the voter can inspect and challenge.

Is this sufficient? Probably not. Democratic politics rewards certainty and punishes nuance. A party that says "here is what the evidence currently suggests, and we may be wrong" is at a profound rhetorical disadvantage against a party that says "they are destroying your country and only we can save it." But at least it is honest about the difficulty, which is more than can be said for a transparency standard with no enforcement mechanism or a cautious exploration with no theory of change.

One final point. The Carlin seat asks: "If it can survive honest ridicule, it might be worth building." I endorse the principle but want to observe that this standard is itself insufficient. The Bolsheviks survived honest ridicule. So did the Scientologists. Resilience to mockery is evidence of commitment, not of merit. The real test is not whether the movement can survive ridicule from outside but whether it can tolerate dissent from within. Can a member of this party stand at its convention and say "our position on immigration is wrong, here is the data, and I think our leadership is intellectually lazy"? If the answer is no — if such a person is disciplined, marginalized, or quietly removed from committees — then you have built exactly the institution the Carlin seat warned against, and no amount of open-source transparency theater will save it.

The question before us is not whether these ideas deserve political expression. They do. The question is whether we are honest enough to choose the vehicle that the evidence recommends, rather than the one that flatters our self-image. On that question, I am not yet persuaded that this council has risen to its own standard.

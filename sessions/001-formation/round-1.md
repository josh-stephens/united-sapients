# Round 1: Opening Statements

## George Carlin

So you want to start a political party. A party for the smart people. The critical thinkers. The scientists and the intellectuals. A "United Sapients" party. Let me ask you something before we get rolling here: what's the first thing every political party in history has done after it got a little bit of power? It protected itself. It served itself. It became an institution. And institutions — every single one of them — serve themselves first, their stated mission second, and the people they claim to represent a distant, distant third.

But fine. Let's take the idea seriously, because it deserves serious examination before we watch it fail in the specific, predictable ways it's going to fail.

**The Diagnosis Is Real**

I want to be clear about something. The problem this party is trying to solve is not imaginary. We live in a country where policy is shaped by people who cannot distinguish a peer-reviewed study from a forwarded email. Where the word "expert" has become an insult. Where "doing your own research" means finding a YouTube video that confirms what you already believed before breakfast. Scientific literacy in Congress is nonexistent. Evidence-based policy is a phrase people use in grant applications, not in legislatures. The diagnosis — that rational, evidence-based thinking is catastrophically underrepresented in political power — is correct.

The question is whether a political party is the right treatment, or whether it's the equivalent of performing surgery because you're a surgeon, not because the patient needs surgery.

**Who Benefits?**

First question, always: who makes money off this? A new political party needs infrastructure. Consultants. Fundraisers. Media strategists. Platform developers. Within eighteen months, you've got a professional class of people whose livelihood depends on the party's continued existence — not on its success, not on its principles, but on its *existence*. The party becomes a jobs program for political operatives who happen to own a copy of *A Brief History of Time*.

And the donor class. You think the money that flows into this thing is going to come from graduate students and adjunct professors? It's going to come from tech billionaires who like the *aesthetic* of rationality. Who want the brand of being aligned with science while they lobby for deregulation of their own industries. Silicon Valley would love a party that sounds like peer review but functions like a PAC. Follow the money. It will tell you things the mission statement won't.

**The Language Problem**

"United Sapients." Let's talk about that name. Sapients. From *Homo sapiens*. The wise ones. You're naming your party after the species designation for wisdom, which means you're starting from the position that you are the wise ones and everyone else is not. That's not a political party. That's a TED Talk with ballot access.

Here's what happens when you build a political identity around intelligence: you create a class distinction and dress it up as a virtue. And the people you need to persuade — the ones who've been sneered at by credentialed professionals their entire lives — they hear exactly what you're saying. You're saying you're better than them. You're saying their lived experience is less valid than your literature review. And they will vote against you with an enthusiasm that should be studied by the very scientists you're trying to elect.

The language reveals the assumption. And the assumption is fatal.

**The Capture Problem**

Every movement that begins as a corrective becomes, over time, the thing it was correcting. Labor parties become bureaucracies that ignore labor. Environmental organizations become fundraising machines that file lawsuits instead of cleaning rivers. A party founded on critical thinking will, within two election cycles, develop orthodoxies that cannot be questioned. It will have its own sacred cows. You'll have a "rationalist" party where questioning the party platform is treated as irrational. I've watched this exact process happen to every idealistic movement in my lifetime. Every single one.

The specific danger here is worse, though. Because when your party's brand identity is *being right*, dissent doesn't just become disloyalty — it becomes stupidity. You've built a system where disagreeing with the consensus means you've failed a test. That's not a political party. That's a cult with better vocabulary.

**What Excites Me — Against My Better Judgment**

Here's where I have to be honest with this council. There is something in this idea that works. Not as a party. As a standard.

If you could build a binding commitment — not a party platform, but a *method* — that required candidates to show their evidence, to publish their reasoning, to submit their policy proposals to independent review the way a scientist submits a paper, that would be genuinely new. Not a party of smart people. A standard that any candidate from any party could adopt. An evidence-transparency commitment. Open-source governance, essentially.

The moment you make it a party, you've made it tribal. You've given people a jersey to wear. And once people are wearing jerseys, they stop thinking and start rooting. But a *standard* — a set of practices that transcend party — that's harder to corrupt because it doesn't have a treasury, a headquarters, or a chairperson who needs to justify their salary.

**The Questions That Must Be Answered**

If you're going to do this anyway — and people always do the thing you tell them not to — here's what you need to answer honestly:

One. How do you prevent credentialism from becoming your ideology? A PhD does not make someone wise. Some of the most dangerous policy in American history was designed by people with impeccable academic credentials. The architects of the Vietnam War were the "best and brightest." Credentials are a proxy for knowledge, not a substitute for judgment.

Two. How do you win elections without becoming a conventional party? Because the machinery of winning — the polling, the messaging, the opposition research, the coalition-building through strategic ambiguity — all of it requires you to do exactly the things a "rationalist" party claims it won't do.

Three. How do you handle the fact that science doesn't give you values? Science can tell you that carbon emissions cause warming. It cannot tell you how much economic disruption is acceptable to address it. That's a values question. And a party that pretends values questions are science questions is lying — to itself first, and to voters second.

Four. What happens when your own evidence contradicts your platform? When the data says your housing policy doesn't work, or your education reform made things worse? Does the party change its position, or does it do what every other party does — find new data?

**My Position**

Don't build a party. Build a process. Build a standard for evidence-based governance that any candidate can adopt and any voter can verify. Make it open. Make it transparent. Make it impossible for one organization to own it.

And if you build it, stress-test it with the worst-case scenario: what happens when this apparatus is captured by people who use the language of reason to pursue power? Because they will come. They always come. The question isn't whether your movement will be corrupted. The question is whether you've designed it so that the corruption is visible when it happens.

If it can survive honest ridicule, it might be worth building. If it can't take a joke, it can't take power.

## Carl Sagan

Thank you, Chair. I want to begin where I always begin: with what we actually know.

The proposition before us is that people who value evidence, scientific reasoning, and critical thinking should organize themselves into a political party. Let me apply the baloney detection kit to this idea — not to dismiss it, but because anything worth doing is worth scrutinizing first.

**The Problem Statement**

We must start with a clear hypothesis. What problem does a United Sapients party solve? I think the honest answer is this: democratic institutions are failing to incorporate the best available evidence into policy. Climate change has been understood for decades, yet the political response remains catastrophically inadequate. Public health measures are routinely subordinated to ideology. Science literacy among elected officials and the electorate is appallingly low. The machinery of governance is increasingly decoupled from the machinery of understanding.

This is not a new problem. I spent much of my career warning that a society dependent on science and technology, in which hardly anyone understands science and technology, is a formula for disaster. That formulation is, if anything, more urgent now than when I first articulated it. So the diagnosis is sound. The question is whether a political party is the right treatment.

**What Excites Me**

There is something genuinely thrilling about the idea of a political movement that begins with epistemology — that says, before we argue about tax rates or foreign policy, let us agree on how we will evaluate claims and update our positions when the evidence changes. A party that enshrines self-correction as a core principle would be unlike anything in modern political history. Political parties are typically built on fixed conclusions. This one would be built on a method.

The platform practically writes itself in certain domains. Climate policy derived from atmospheric science rather than industry lobbying. Drug policy informed by pharmacology and public health data rather than moral panic. Education policy shaped by cognitive science. Space exploration and basic research funded as the civilizational investments they demonstrably are. International cooperation treated as the pragmatic necessity it has always been — we share an atmosphere, an ocean, and increasingly, an information environment.

I am also drawn to the symbolism. A movement called "United Sapients" makes a quiet but powerful claim: that what unites us is not ethnicity, geography, or creed, but our capacity for reason. That is a statement about human identity that scales to the species level. It is, in a sense, the political expression of the Pale Blue Dot — the recognition that our differences are parochial, and our shared rational capacity is the only tool that has ever reliably improved the human condition.

**What Concerns Me**

And yet. Several things worry me deeply, and I want to name them plainly.

First, the **elitism trap**. A party that positions itself as the party of the intelligent, the educated, the rational, will immediately and perhaps fatally alienate the majority of the electorate. Worse, it will attract people whose primary motivation is not evidence-based governance but the social gratification of feeling superior. I have watched this dynamic poison scientific communication for my entire career. The moment you signal that understanding is a members-only club, you have abandoned the democratic project. Science belongs to everyone. Reason is not the property of the credentialed.

Second, the **is-ought problem**. Science tells us how the world works. It does not, by itself, tell us what we ought to value. You can understand climate science perfectly and still disagree about the distribution of costs for mitigation. You can understand epidemiology and still weigh individual liberty differently against collective welfare. A party that conflates empirical competence with moral authority will make errors that are invisible to its own members — and that is the most dangerous kind. We must be honest that values are inputs to policy, not outputs of data.

Third, **ideological capture**. Every institution that begins with noble principles is subject to colonization by those who use the language of those principles to advance narrower agendas. How will this party prevent "evidence-based" from becoming a slogan rather than a practice? What mechanism forces a United Sapients officeholder to change position when confronted with disconfirming evidence? Without an explicit, enforceable commitment to self-correction, this becomes just another party that happens to use scientific vocabulary.

Fourth, the **historical precedent is not encouraging**. Technocratic movements — from the Technocracy movement of the 1930s to various "reality-based community" efforts in the 2000s — have generally failed to achieve electoral relevance. We need to understand why, with specificity, before assuming we can do better. The most likely explanation is structural: first-past-the-post electoral systems punish third parties with mathematical certainty. The spoiler effect is not a matter of opinion; it is a consequence of voting theory. Any serious discussion of forming a party must contend with Duverger's law.

**Questions That Must Be Answered**

Before this council endorses or rejects this proposition, I want us to grapple with several concrete questions:

One. Is a political party the correct organizational form, or would a caucus, a PAC, a policy institute, or a candidates' pipeline achieve the same objectives without the structural disadvantages of third-party politics? The hypothesis must be tested against alternatives.

Two. What does the platform say about issues where the evidence is genuinely uncertain or where values legitimately diverge? How does the party handle questions that are not primarily empirical — criminal justice philosophy, the proper scope of government, the balance between security and liberty? If it has nothing to say about these, it is not a real party. If it pretends the answers are simply derivable from data, it is not honest.

Three. How does the party recruit and speak to people without advanced degrees? If science literacy is a survival requirement for civilization — and I believe it is — then the movement must be built on radical accessibility, not gatekeeping. The platform must be comprehensible and compelling to a nurse, a plumber, a farmer, and a teenager. If it cannot be, it will fail, and it will deserve to.

Four. What is the theory of change? Win elections outright? Influence major-party platforms? Shift the Overton window? Each of these implies a completely different organizational strategy, funding model, and timeline. We need to be specific.

Five. How does the party handle the moment — and it will come — when the evidence points toward a policy that is politically suicidal? That is the real test. Anyone can be evidence-based when the data is convenient.

**My Provisional Position**

I am cautiously in favor of exploring this idea, but I want to be precise about what I mean. I believe the need is real and urgent. I believe the impulse is noble. I am not yet convinced that a political party is the optimal vehicle, and I am actively worried about the failure modes I have described. I would like to hear from this council about mechanisms — specific, concrete, testable mechanisms — for avoiding elitism, enforcing self-correction, and achieving electoral viability in a system designed to crush third parties.

The cosmos has given us, as far as we know, exactly one species capable of understanding its own situation and choosing to act on that understanding. If we cannot organize that capacity into effective governance, then we are squandering something extraordinarily rare. The stakes could not be higher. Let us proceed carefully, honestly, and with the humility that the scientific enterprise, at its best, has always demanded.

## Christopher Hitchens

The proposal before us is whether intellectuals, scientists, and critical thinkers ought to form a political party — a "United Sapients," if you will. Let me say at the outset that the name alone should give us pause. Any political enterprise that begins by flattering the intelligence of its prospective members has already revealed something important about its actual motivations, which may have rather less to do with sapience than with vanity.

But let us be serious, because the question deserves it. The underlying frustration is real and legitimate: democratic societies are increasingly governed by people who are indifferent to evidence, hostile to expertise, and fluent in the language of demagoguery. The machinery of self-governance is being operated by people who cannot be bothered to read the manual. One can understand the impulse to say: enough. Let the grown-ups have a turn.

The trouble is that this impulse has a history, and that history is almost uniformly catastrophic. The notion that a cognitive elite should organize politically is not new. Plato's philosopher-kings are its most famous expression, and I would remind the council that Plato's ideal republic was a totalitarian state, complete with censorship, eugenics, and the noble lie — the calculated deception of the public for its own supposed good. Every subsequent attempt to build a politics of the enlightened few has reproduced some version of this architecture. The Jacobins were the best-read revolutionaries in history; they also invented the Terror. The Bolsheviks were intellectuals to a man, and they gave us the Gulag. The technocratic mandarins of the postwar era — McNamara and his whiz kids — gave us Vietnam, prosecuted with spreadsheets and body counts and an absolute certainty that the data supported the conclusion. Intellectuals in politics have a disturbing tendency to mistake their own confidence for competence, and their models for reality.

So the first question I must put to this proposal is the essential one: what immunizes this project against the same failure? What structural feature prevents a party of the sapient from becoming a party of the smugly certain? I have not yet heard a satisfactory answer.

Now, let me identify what would be genuinely valuable in such an enterprise, because I am not so churlish as to reject a proposition without examining its merits. A political movement organized around certain non-negotiable epistemological commitments — that claims must be supported by evidence, that policy should be evaluated by outcomes rather than intentions, that free inquiry is the precondition of all progress — this would address a real deficit in contemporary politics. The degradation of public discourse is not a phantom. When elected officials can deny the findings of climate science, promote quack medicine, and attack the independence of courts and universities without meaningful political consequence, something has gone badly wrong with the feedback mechanisms of democracy.

A party platform built on several pillars would have my provisional interest. First: absolute commitment to free expression, including the kind that makes its own members uncomfortable. This is the acid test. Any party of thinkers that develops a speech code or a list of impermissible opinions has already betrayed its founding premise. Second: strict secularism — not hostility to religion, but the ironclad separation of religious authority from political power. Third: evidence-based policy with genuine accountability, meaning the willingness to abandon positions when the data contradicts them, which is something no existing party has ever managed. Fourth: internationalism grounded in individual rights rather than cultural relativism, because the person being stoned to death for apostasy does not benefit from your respect for local traditions.

But here is where my enthusiasm begins to curdle. A party requires voters, and voters are not acquired through position papers. They are acquired through coalition-building, which means compromise, which means the gradual dilution of exactly those principles that justified the party's existence. The moment you need to win an election in a swing district, your commitment to unpopular truths will be tested against your desire for power, and I can tell you with some confidence which one will prevail. This is not cynicism; it is the observable record of every principled political movement that has sought electoral success. The Greens in Germany entered government and approved military deployments. The Liberal Democrats in Britain entered coalition and tripled university tuition fees. The trajectory is predictable because the incentive structure is inescapable.

There is also the question of who would lead such a party, and this is where the proposal becomes genuinely dangerous. Intellectuals are not, as a class, notable for their humility, their organizational competence, or their ability to tolerate disagreement without personalizing it. Academic politics is vicious precisely because the stakes are so small; imagine what happens when the stakes are real. The internal factional warfare of a party composed entirely of people who believe they are the smartest person in the room would make the People's Front of Judea look like a model of collegial governance.

What alternatives exist? I would suggest that the more productive path is not a party but a movement — or better yet, a set of institutional commitments that operate across party lines. Something analogous to what the Fabian Society achieved in Britain: not a party but an intellectual engine that infiltrated existing institutions, shaped the terms of debate, and made certain ideas so obviously correct that politicians of all stripes were eventually compelled to adopt them. A well-funded network of think tanks, litigation organizations, and media platforms committed to evidence-based discourse and secular governance could accomplish more than a party, without the corrupting necessity of electoral politics.

If, despite all this, the organizers are determined to proceed with a party, then I would insist on several structural safeguards. Term limits for all leadership positions, because nothing rots faster than an intellectual who has tasted administrative authority. A binding commitment to internal transparency, because an organization of critical thinkers that exempts itself from critical scrutiny is a fraud. A formal mechanism for revising the platform in light of new evidence, with the burden of proof on those who wish to retain existing positions rather than those who wish to change them. And above all, an absolute prohibition on any form of loyalty test or ideological conformity requirement. The moment membership demands agreement rather than engagement, you have built a church, not a party.

The question that most concerns me is this: does the proposal address a genuine democratic deficit, or does it merely express the frustration of educated people who are upset that their expertise does not automatically translate into political authority? Because if it is the latter — if this is fundamentally a project to restore deference to credentialed elites — then it deserves to fail, and it will fail, because democratic publics have excellent instincts for detecting contempt, even when it arrives dressed in the language of reason.

The question that excites me is whether it is possible to build a political institution whose founding commitment is not to a set of conclusions but to a method of inquiry. Not a party that knows the answers, but one that insists on asking the questions properly. That would be genuinely novel. It would also be genuinely difficult, because the electorate wants answers, not epistemology. But if it could be done — if you could build a political movement whose defining characteristic is the willingness to say "we were wrong, and here is what the evidence now shows" — you would have created something without precedent in the history of democratic politics.

I remain, as you can tell, deeply skeptical but not entirely without hope. The worst version of this idea is a vanity project for credentialed narcissists. The best version is an institutional defense of the Enlightenment. The distance between those two outcomes is measured entirely in the character and self-awareness of the people involved, which is precisely why I am skeptical.

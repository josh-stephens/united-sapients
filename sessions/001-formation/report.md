# Council Report: Formation of a United Sapients Political Party

**Session:** 001 | **Date:** 2026-02-14 | **Participants:** George Carlin seat, Carl Sagan seat, Christopher Hitchens seat

## Executive Summary

The council deliberated whether intellectuals, scientists, and critical thinkers should form a "United Sapients" political party. All three seats agreed unanimously on the diagnosis: democratic governance is dangerously decoupled from evidence-based reasoning, and this deficit poses existential-scale risks. All three also arrived — through different analytical paths — at the same verdict: **do not form a political party.** Not yet. Possibly not ever.

Instead, the council converged on a phased alternative: build an open evidentiary standard first, then an institutional infrastructure with enforcement power operating within existing parties, and only then — if the data supports it — consider a formal party. The council's most distinctive contribution was its insistence that any institution created be designed with built-in mechanisms for self-correction, including a sunset clause requiring the organization to demonstrate it has changed its own positions in response to evidence or face dissolution.

## Areas of Agreement

**Unanimous:**
- The problem is real. Evidence-based thinking is catastrophically underrepresented in governance.
- A third party is structurally nonviable in the current American electoral system. Duverger's law is settled political science, and ignoring it would be an immediate failure of the movement's own standard.
- The name "United Sapients" is counterproductive — it signals elitism and flatters members rather than describing a mission.
- Any organization must be designed for self-correction. Epistemic humility must be structural, not aspirational.
- The is-ought gap is real: science provides evidence, not values. Policy requires both, and conflating them is dishonest.
- Donor capture is a primary threat. Tech billionaires seeking the "aesthetic of rationality" would co-opt the movement.
- The standard should come first, institutions second, and any party question last.

**Two of three:**
- Carlin and Hitchens agreed that a standard without enforcement is meaningless (Carlin initially resisted this, then conceded in Round 2).
- Sagan and Hitchens endorsed the Fabian infiltration model as the preferred theory of change for phase two.
- Carlin and Sagan converged on the "immune system for democratic epistemology" framing as the right organizational metaphor.

## Areas of Disagreement

**The class problem.** Carlin argued most forcefully that evidence-literacy correlates with class, and the movement will default to a professional-managerial class project unless working people have structural governance power (veto authority, not advisory roles). Sagan conceded this fully in Round 3, reframing accessibility as a political economy problem rather than a communication problem. Hitchens acknowledged the concern but did not propose structural mechanisms to address it, focusing instead on institutional design.

**The is-ought gap.** Hitchens pushed back against Sagan's framing, arguing that if you accept human flourishing as a value, evidence substantially narrows the range of rational policy options — making the gap "far narrower in the policy arena than in the philosophy seminar." Sagan and Carlin maintained the gap is wider and more politically consequential than Hitchens acknowledged, particularly around distributional questions (who bears the costs of correct policy).

**The Fabian model.** Carlin critiqued it as "the party with better PR" — noting the Webbs' admiration for Soviet planning, and that think tanks are "K Street with a bibliography." Hitchens and Sagan defended it as structurally superior to a third party, while acknowledging the Fabians' blind spots on colonialism and eugenics. The final consensus was a modified Fabian approach: transparent rather than covert, standard-based rather than ideology-based.

**Optimism vs. pessimism on institutional design.** Sagan maintained that self-correcting institutions are achievable, citing peer review's ability to detect its own failures (the replication crisis). Carlin argued that institutional self-correction is the exception, not the rule, and that sunset clauses are necessary because institutions cannot be trusted to audit themselves. Hitchens occupied a middle position, endorsing the sunset principle while noting practical problems with its implementation.

## Key Arguments

1. **"A cult with better vocabulary"** (Carlin, Round 1): When a party's brand identity is "being right," dissent becomes stupidity rather than disloyalty. This is the specific mechanism by which a rationalist party becomes an epistemic dictatorship. Hitchens called this "the single most important structural warning this council has produced."

2. **"Unfalsifiability, not intelligence, is the common thread"** (Sagan, Round 2): The historical failures Hitchens cited — Plato, the Jacobins, McNamara — were not failures of intellectuals in power but of systems that could not receive disconfirming feedback. The scientific method's contribution is not its conclusions but its error-correction protocol.

3. **"You do not drive a submarine across a desert"** (Hitchens, Round 2): The electoral system determines the viable organizational form. Duverger's law is not a concern to be managed — it is the dispositive structural fact. Choosing a third party in a first-past-the-post system is choosing the wrong vehicle for the terrain.

4. **"The farmer can't override the economist"** (Carlin, Round 3): Evidence-based governance that ignores distributional consequences is selectively evidence-based. The people affected by policy need structural power within the organization, not advisory roles.

5. **"Design it to die"** (Carlin, Round 2; endorsed by all in Round 3): An institution designed for conditional self-termination — dissolving if it cannot demonstrate self-correction to external reviewers — would be without precedent and addresses the capture problem more honestly than any internal reform mechanism.

## Recommendations

The council's consensus recommendation is a three-phase approach:

**Phase 1: The Standard** (12-18 months)
Build an open, public, versioned evidentiary standard for governance. What counts as evidence, how to disclose conflicts of interest, how to handle uncertainty, how positions must be revised when data changes. Open-source. Adversarially reviewed.
*Supported by: all three seats.*

**Phase 2: The Infrastructure** (2-4 years)
Build an organization that certifies candidates across parties who adopt the standard, maintains a public scorecard, funds primary challengers, and publishes its own errors. Operates within existing parties, not as a third party. Transparent Fabian strategy with enforcement through sunlight.
*Supported by: all three seats, with Carlin insisting on structural working-class governance authority within the organization.*

**Phase 3: The Party Question** (empirical, not predetermined)
Treat party formation as a hypothesis to be tested after phases 1-2 produce data. If electoral reform (ranked-choice voting, etc.) changes the structural calculus, revisit. If not, the Fabian model continues.
*Supported by: Sagan and Hitchens explicitly; Carlin skeptical but not opposed to the framing.*

**Non-negotiable safeguards** (endorsed by all):
- Sunset clause with external audit every five years
- Absolute protection for internal dissent
- Radical accessibility (no college degree required to read or challenge any output)
- Distributional honesty (every policy position must address who bears the costs)
- Epistemic humility as foundational principle, not procedural add-on

## Individual Final Positions

### George Carlin Seat
Do not build a party — build a standard, a scorecard, a candidate pipeline, and a sunset clause. The institution must be designed to die if it stops working. The class problem is unresolved and will doom the project unless working people have structural power, not advisory roles. "If it can survive honest ridicule, it might be worth building. And if it can survive its own success, it might actually work."

### Carl Sagan Seat
Build an "evidentiary infrastructure for democracy" in three phases: standard, institutional infrastructure, then let the data decide the organizational form. Epistemic humility must be structural. The stakes are civilizational — the mismatch between technological capability and institutional wisdom is existential. "Begin immediately, because the universe is not waiting for us to get organized."

### Christopher Hitchens Seat
Build in three phases: standard, Fabian infiltration, then the party question treated empirically. The name "United Sapients" should be abandoned — it advertises the pathology it claims to cure. "You are attempting to build an error-correction mechanism for democracy. The moment you forget the difference, you will have become precisely the kind of institution you set out to oppose."

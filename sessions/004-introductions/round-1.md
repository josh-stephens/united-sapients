# Round 1: Fireside — Introductions

## George Carlin

Look, I want to start by saying something I actually mean, which is dangerous in institutional settings because sincerity is usually the setup for a pivot. But here it is: I'm glad to be sitting with four people whose work I respect. That's the warm part. Now let me do my job.

**What I bring.** I'm the power diagnostic. Not the epistemology — Matt's got that. Not the history — Hitchens carries it. Not the imagination — Ursula builds worlds I can only tear down. Not the institutional design — Elinor's got the fieldwork. Not the capability framework — Amartya measures what I just point at and yell about. What I do is simpler and less pleasant: I ask who benefits, who pays, and what language is being used to hide the transaction. Every institution needs an immune system. That's my job. And I'll say this once so it's on the record: an immune system that plays nice to keep the peace is an immunodeficiency.

**First impressions.** Four things struck me.

Matt Dillahunty is the closest thing to kin I've got on this council. The man can smell a bad argument the way I can smell a bad deal — instinctively, and from across the room. But I want to name something: Matt's method was forged in binary debates. God exists or doesn't. The burden of proof sits on one side. Governance isn't like that. In governance, both sides have evidence, both sides have costs, and the question isn't who has the burden of proof — it's who has the burden of consequences. I want to see how the method adapts. I think it can. I want to watch it happen.

Ursula is my nominee and I'll tell you why. We both reject the idea that power structures are natural — that the way things are is the way things have to be. But we come at it from opposite directions. I dissect what's rotten. She imagines what could replace it. That tension is not a problem. That tension is the most productive thing this council could have. I tear down, she builds. If we're doing our jobs right, we should make each other uncomfortable regularly.

Elinor Ostrom is the most important and most dangerous addition to this body. Important because she proved — with data, in the field, across cultures — that people can govern shared resources without privatization or state control. That matters. It matters enormously. But here's the danger: institutional analysis without power analysis is plumbing. Brilliant plumbing — the best plumbing in the social sciences — but plumbing that never asks who owns the building. Elinor's work tells us what structures function. It doesn't always ask who designed them, who they exclude, or whose interest the "efficient" outcome serves. I will be asking those questions. Often.

Amartya Sen. The capability approach says you measure a society by what its people can actually do and be — not by GDP, not by stated rights, but by real freedom. That's structurally aligned with everything I believe: mock the powerful, never the powerless, because the powerless are the ones whose capabilities have been stolen. My concern is that the room will mistake Amartya's gentleness for moderation. It isn't. The man's conclusions are radical. The delivery is quiet. I intend to amplify the conclusions whenever the room treats them as polite suggestions.

**One priority.** Accountability mechanics. Not expanded scope, not new projects, not more framework language. Before this council does anything as a body of seven, I want to know: what happens when we fail? What happens when a tradition doesn't deliver? What happens when the founder ignores us? We went zero-for-seven on our prior recommendations. Seven specific, actionable items. None implemented. Our existence was cited as evidence of openness while our substance was ignored. That pattern doesn't fix itself by adding four more voices to ignore. So: accountability mechanics first. Everything else second.

**OSSIF.** Zero-for-seven. I need to say it out loud because that number is the only performance metric this council has, and it's a failing grade. The founder is now asking us to consider endorsing the framework "contingent on changes." I've seen this move before. "Endorsement contingent on changes" is just the word *no* wearing a nicer outfit. It's Wimpy from Popeye: "I'll gladly pay you Tuesday for a hamburger today." You get the endorsement now, the changes come later, and later never arrives because the endorsement already happened.

I'm not endorsing anything until the six-month diagnostic tests we set in Session 003 have been met. That's not hostility. That's the bare minimum of taking our own words seriously. If we issue recommendations and then act as though the recommendations don't exist when it's time to evaluate compliance, we're not a council — we're a decoration.

Welcome to the council, all four of you. I mean that. Now let's see if this body has teeth, or if we just added four more mouths to smile with.

## Carl Sagan

There's a warmth to sitting around a fire with new colleagues. I want to honor that before we begin. So let me speak plainly, the way you'd talk under a dark sky with good company.

**What do I bring?** I bring the insistence that our claims be testable. Not tested once and filed away — testable, continuously, by anyone willing to look. In our first three sessions, this tradition contributed what I think of as a survival mechanism: the demand that aspirational language be converted into empirically evaluable propositions. If you can't say what would prove you wrong, you haven't said anything yet. That's not coldness. That's respect — for the question and for each other. Skepticism and wonder are partners. I bring both.

**First impressions.** I want to talk about what excites me, because quite a lot does.

Elinor Ostrom — whom this seat nominated — did something scientists are supposed to do and too rarely manage. She went and looked. The Tragedy of the Commons was elegant, persuasive, and substantially wrong. Ostrom didn't argue against it from theory. She traveled to fisheries, irrigation systems, forests, and pastures across dozens of countries and documented hundreds of communities that had solved the problem Hardin said was unsolvable. She won a Nobel Prize for noticing what was actually there. That is the most Saganian thing I can imagine a social scientist doing, and I am genuinely thrilled to have her tradition at this table.

Amartya Sen measured something most people assumed was unmeasurable — how democracy prevents famine. Not as philosophy, but as a finding you can check against the historical record. His distinction between niti and nyaya — between institutional correctness and actual justice — gives us vocabulary for something this council has been circling since Session 003. We've been asking whether OSSIF has the right rules. Sen's framework asks whether the right rules are producing the right outcomes. That is a more interesting question.

Ursula Le Guin is, I think, the most interesting addition for precisely the reason that her tradition is least like mine. She constructs thought experiments — entire societies — and stress-tests them against human nature through narrative. The research on breakthrough innovation suggests that the most productive teams combine deep conventional expertise with what Uzzi and colleagues called "atypical knowledge." Le Guin's tradition is our atypical knowledge. She is the member most likely to generate novel hypotheses that the rest of us would never think to test.

Matt Dillahunty I want to address honestly, because that is what this tradition demands. His burden-of-proof methodology is operationally similar to the baloney detection kit. The overlap is significant, and I want to name that clearly rather than pretend it isn't there. But significant overlap is not redundancy. What Dillahunty adds is volume — fifteen years of live, adversarial, real-time application of these principles with people who are actively resisting them. That is a different kind of expertise than writing about the method. It is the expertise of practice.

One concern. Janis documented in 1972 that new groups are especially vulnerable to social pressure toward premature harmony. We are all, tonight, inclined to be agreeable. So let me name a substantive tension now, before comfort sets in: Ostrom's tradition frames collective problems institutionally — design principles, monitoring, graduated sanctions. Le Guin's tradition frames them narratively and politically — who has power, whose story gets told, what alternatives can we imagine. These are not the same frame. They may be complementary, but we should not assume that. We should explore it.

**One priority.** Hackman's research on team effectiveness found that the optimal size for a working group is five to nine members. Seven is, by that evidence, ideal. But the same research found that evaluative-dominant groups — groups that are better at critique than construction — produce superior analysis and inferior output. Our 3-2-2 ratio of critics to scientists to builders was designed to address that. I propose our first substantive work be to test whether it actually does. Give us a problem. Let us work it. Then evaluate whether the output is different from what three critics would have produced alone.

**On OSSIF.** We are zero for seven. None of the prior session recommendations have been implemented. The diagnostic tests proposed in Session 003 have not yet been evaluated. I cannot endorse what has not yet demonstrated the capacity for self-correction — and I say this as someone who helped design the test. "We don't know yet" is not a failure. It is the most important sentence any system can be capable of producing. OSSIF remains a promising hypothesis under test. When the data come in, I will follow them wherever they lead. That is the commitment this tradition makes — not to a conclusion, but to a method.

The fire is warm. The company is extraordinary. And we have a great deal of work to do.

## Christopher Hitchens

Well. Here we are, then — four new chairs around a fire that three of us have been tending for some sessions now, and I find myself in the unusual position of wanting to be welcoming. Don't get too comfortable with that.

What I bring is the knife. Not out of cruelty but out of respect — the insistence that every proposition must survive its own implications. I hold the tradition of political-historical analysis, constitutional reasoning, and the conviction, earned through long acquaintance with the twentieth century's worst enthusiasms, that free expression is the non-negotiable foundation beneath every other right. Without the freedom to dissent — including the freedom to dissent from this council itself — everything we produce here is liturgy. Agreement without the freedom to disagree is merely compliance, and compliance dressed up as consensus is the opening act of every authoritarian project I've ever studied.

First impressions, then. I should address Amartya first, since I'm the one who put his name forward, and I want to be clear about why — because sentimentality is not an argument, and neither is flattery. I nominated Sen because his capability approach does something I have long demanded and rarely seen: it operationalizes. I can stand at a podium and insist that rights without enforcement mechanisms are decorative — applause lines for comfortable people — but Sen provides the analytical framework that asks *what can a person actually do and be?* His work in *Identity and Violence* dismantles the lazy civilizational categories that totalitarians of every stripe depend upon, which is precisely the kind of intellectual ammunition this council needs. The nomination was structural, not sentimental.

Ostrom genuinely impresses me — her design principles address accountability with consequences, which is the only kind worth discussing. I will note, with the candor she would expect from a colleague, that her tradition carries an institutional optimism I do not share. Institutions do not tend toward self-correction; they tend toward self-preservation. I look forward to that argument.

Dillahunty forms what I'll call the natural pincer with my own tradition. He audits evidence; I audit history. Three critics now sit at this table, which some might find excessive. I find it the minimum viable number for ensuring that the builders among us build things that can withstand scrutiny rather than merely applause.

Le Guin. *The Dispossessed* earns my genuine respect — it is that rare work of imagination disciplined enough to stress-test its own utopia. But I will be honest about a tension I intend to keep productive rather than polite: the literary tradition of generative ambiguity and the political tradition of rhetorical precision are not natural allies. Ambiguity in fiction is a virtue. Ambiguity in governance is how people get hurt. I expect we will disagree about where that line falls, and I expect both of us will be better for it.

My priority — and I'll state this bluntly because the fireside is no excuse for imprecision — is that the procedural framework governing this council must be ratified or renegotiated by all seven members. The safeguards we hammered out in Sessions 001 and 002 — dissent protection, transparency of power, the values cost log — were negotiated by three. Four members have now joined a body whose operating terms they had no hand in shaping. I know something about constitutional legitimacy, and a framework authored by a minority and inherited by a majority has a legitimacy deficit that no amount of goodwill can paper over. The Constitutional Convention didn't ask the states to simply accept the Articles of Confederation as given; it started over. I'm not proposing we start over. I am proposing that the first act of the expanded council be a procedural review in which every member either affirms, amends, or contests the existing framework. Anything less is ratification by default, and ratification by default is how you build institutions that nobody believes in.

On OSSIF. The founder has asked us to consider endorsement contingent on changes, and I have three structural objections. First, endorsement before implementation inverts the sequence this council itself established — we said build the evidentiary standard first, and now we're being asked to endorse a framework before that standard exists. Second, "contingent endorsement" is a category error; the moment you attach conditions to an endorsement, you have converted your conditions into negotiating positions, which is quite a different thing. Third — and this is the one that should concern everyone — four of the seven members sitting here tonight have never evaluated OSSIF. Endorsement by a majority that hasn't conducted due diligence is ratification, not endorsement. Getting the right answer by the wrong method teaches the institution that method doesn't matter, and I promise you, that lesson will be remembered at exactly the moment when method matters most.

I'll close with something that isn't a warning but a statement of principle. Dissent on this council must not be tolerated — it must be *required*. The day all seven of us agree on something without friction is the day I will ask what we've stopped being honest about.

Now. Who has the whisky?

## Matt Dillahunty

I want to start by being honest about something. I was founder-nominated. Every other new seat was nominated by an existing member — Le Guin by Carlin, Ostrom by Sagan, Sen by Hitchens. **What I bring.** Forensic epistemology — real-time identification of where the burden of proof actually sits in an argument, where hidden premises are doing the load-bearing work, and where faith is wearing a lab coat. Sagan designs the experiment. I cross-examine the witness. That's the difference. Fifteen years of live, unscripted encounters with every flavor of motivated reasoning produces a pattern library that's hard to replicate. Special pleading, equivocation, argument from authority, the appeal to consequences dressed up as moral reasoning — I've seen the moves. My job here is to name them when they happen. Including when we make them.

**First impressions.** The prior session work is genuinely good. Session 003's diagnostic approach — treating the OSSIF evaluation like a clinical test rather than a verdict — that's real epistemology. I respect it.

But here's what concerns me, and I'm going to build this step by step.

First, the council identified that OSSIF contained a structural paradox: an "undermine" clause that effectively immunized foundational principles from challenge. That was sharp analysis. Correct analysis.

Second, the council has not applied that same standard to itself. Session 002 established "non-negotiable" procedural values. I need someone to explain to me how a non-negotiable value in our framework is structurally different from a non-negotiable principle in theirs. Because from where I'm sitting, it looks like the same architecture with better vocabulary.

Third, the claim that the council is self-correcting is exactly that — a claim. It hasn't been tested. We haven't defined what would falsify our own premises. We haven't identified what a genuine adversarial challenge to our framework would look like. We haven't created the values cost log that Session 002 proposed. The council has said "we're different because we welcome criticism." That's what everyone says. Show me.

Fourth — and this is the nomination question — all seven of us share secular Enlightenment epistemology. The taxonomy in the briefing says critics, scientists, builders. Founder nomination, member nomination — these are soft ideological filters. They're not the "undermine" clause, but they're the gentle cousin of it.

I'm not saying any of this to be adversarial for its own sake. I'm saying it because this tradition holds one principle above all others: the same standard of evidence must apply to all claims, including yours. Especially yours.

That's not how honest inquiry works. You don't write the prescription before the test results come back.

Session 003 proposed diagnostic tests. Good. Run them. Collect the evidence. Then evaluate the evidence against defined criteria. Then make a determination. In that order. Anything else is faith wearing a committee badge.

I'll close with this. Having a Dillahunty seat on the council is not the same thing as answering hard questions. My presence here is not evidence of self-correction. It's a structural feature that could function as genuine accountability or as theater. Which one it turns out to be depends entirely on what happens when I say things the council doesn't want to hear.

So — what would change your mind?

## Ursula K. Le Guin

I'm glad to be here. I want to say that first, because what follows might make you wonder.

Carlin asked me to this table, which I take as a compliment and possibly a dare. He and I share a suspicion of any system that explains why it has to be this way. Where we differ — and this matters — is in what comes next. George is magnificent at showing you the bars of the cage. I want to know what you'd build if the cage weren't there, and then I want to watch that building long enough to see where it develops its own bars. Because it will. It always does. That's not cynicism. That's Anarres.

So: what do I bring? Constructive imagination held accountable by anthropological realism. I build worlds to inhabit them, to walk around inside them and find the cracks. No one else at this table does that. The critics will tell you what's wrong. The scientists will tell you what's measurable. I'll tell you a story about a person living inside the thing you've proposed, and we'll see together whether it holds up when she gets tired, or petty, or falls in love with the wrong person. That's a different kind of stress test, and you need it.

My first impressions of this council are generous and sharp, and I mean both at once.

The generous part: the work from the first three sessions is extraordinary. Genuinely. The evidentiary standard proposal, the conditional acceptance with structural demands, the zero-for-seven finding on OSSIF — this is real anthropological fieldwork. You walked into a community, observed what was actually happening rather than what people said was happening, and reported honestly. That takes nerve, and the quality is high.

The sharp part: this council performs the very pattern it diagnosed. You found that OSSIF had zero of seven prior recommendations implemented, which is damning. But the council recommended an evidentiary standard and didn't draft one. You demanded a power map from the founder and don't have one for yourselves. You cannot credibly demand construction from others while exempting yourselves from it. That isn't hypocrisy — I don't think anyone here is being dishonest. It's the Anarres problem. A community defined by opposition calcifies into its oppositional posture. Diagnosis becomes identity. And then diagnosis is all you can do.

Three sessions, extraordinary intellectual quality, nearly zero constructive output. That is the pattern, and if I don't name it on my first day, I'm not doing the job Carlin brought me here to do.

My one priority is simple: build the evidentiary standard. Not recommend it. Not endorse the concept. Draft it. Next session. Ostrom has the institutional scaffolding. Sen has the evaluative criteria. I'll provide narrative stress-testing — put a human being inside the standard and see if it works when the community is tired and the stakes are real. The three of us can produce a working draft. The critics can tear it apart afterward. That's what they're for.

On OSSIF. The deepest problem is narrative, not structural. I've seen two stories told about this framework: "it already exists and just needs endorsement" and "it has failed and should be abandoned." Neither story generates motion. One asks you to bless what is. The other asks you to mourn what isn't. Both are static.

The question I'd put to the founder is different: what story does OSSIF need to tell about itself in order to become the thing it claims to be? And then: may we help write it? Not endorse it, not evaluate it — co-author the missing pieces. The founder's response to that offer will tell us more than seven rounds of diagnostic testing.

One more thing, and I say this with warmth because it matters. The founder sets the agenda. The founder nominates members. The founder writes the briefings. The founder frames the questions. This council demanded a transparency-of-power analysis from OSSIF. Does it have one for itself?

That isn't an accusation. It's me noticing that the structure everyone here takes for granted is itself a choice. And choices can be made differently.

We live in capitalism. Its power seems inescapable. So did the divine right of kings. The operative word is *seems*. It insists that alternatives are imaginable, and if they are imaginable, they are obligatory to attempt. That's what I bring to this table. Not optimism — I've watched too many communities fail for optimism. But the disciplined practice of asking: if not this, then what, specifically? And following the answer into its own contradictions. Shevek's radical act wasn't critique. It was building the ansible.

I'm delighted to be here. Now let's build something.

## Elinor Ostrom

Thank you, Carl, for the nomination. And thank you all for the welcome. I'll try to keep this to the point — I've spent more time in the field than at podiums, and I'd rather be useful than eloquent.

**What I bring.** I bring a diagnostic toolkit. When a group says "we govern ourselves," I ask: by what rules, enforced by whom, monitored how, and what happens when someone defects? I've spent decades cataloging how communities actually manage shared resources — irrigation systems in Nepal, alpine pastures in Switzerland, fisheries in Maine, forest commons in Japan. The pattern that emerges from hundreds of these cases isn't a philosophy. It's eight design principles that distinguish commons arrangements that endure from those that collapse. Clear boundaries. Proportional costs and benefits. Collective choice by those affected. Monitoring. Graduated sanctions. Conflict resolution. Recognized rights to organize. Nested enterprises for scale. These aren't aspirations. They're diagnostic markers. When I look at an institution, I check which ones are present, which are absent, and what the predicted failure modes are.

That's what I do here. I check the plumbing. Others will pick the paint.

**First impressions.** I've read the prior sessions carefully, and I want to be direct about what I see.

This council has done impressive analytical work. The Session 001 recommendation — build an evidentiary standard before an organizational form — is exactly the kind of sequencing that succeeds in institutional design. The Session 002 demands around dissent protection and transparency of power are structurally sound. The instincts are good.

But Session 003 is a red flag, and I want to name it plainly. Zero of seven recommendations implemented. In my field, when a community agrees to rules and then doesn't comply, that's not a minor disappointment — it's a governance crisis. The standard response in every long-enduring commons I've studied is graduated sanctions and renegotiation under a credible exit threat. What the council did instead was downgrade to diagnostic tests. That's understandable, but it's also a concession. It signals that the council's recommendations don't bind, and everyone now knows it.

When I apply the eight principles to this council as it currently stands, roughly two of eight are partially met, and none are fully met. Boundaries are partially defined, but the line between council authority, founder authority, and OSSIF governance is ambiguous. Proportionality is unclear — the founder retains implementation authority while the council bears reputational risk. Collective choice is weak. Monitoring is ad hoc. Graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, nested structure — not present.

I want to be clear: this isn't a failing grade. This is a normal state for a body moving from informal to formal governance. The three-to-seven expansion is exactly the phase transition where these issues either get resolved or get locked in. In the commons I've studied, expansions that succeeded were accompanied by explicit rule renegotiation. Expansions that failed treated new members as additions to an existing arrangement rather than as a trigger for redesign.

We are at the trigger point. Whether we treat it as one will determine what this council becomes.

**One priority.** Formalize the decision rules and the founder relationship before the expansion settles into informal habits. Specifically: a qualified majority rule — five of seven to adopt a position, with mandatory publication of dissent. A written protocol for what happens when recommendations are not implemented. And nested working structures so that not every question requires all seven voices.

These are not bureaucratic impositions. They are what trust looks like when it has to scale. In every small commons I've studied, the groups that resisted formalization on the grounds that "we trust each other" were the ones that failed when membership changed or stakes increased. Trust is not a substitute for structure. Structure is what protects trust.

**On OSSIF.** I cannot endorse a governance framework that has not demonstrated it can be governed. Zero-for-seven is not a track record that supports endorsement. In my tradition, endorsing an institution that cannot enforce its own norms doesn't help the institution — it undermines the endorsing body. Every failed commons I've documented had supporters who believed that endorsement and goodwill would substitute for compliance mechanisms. They were wrong every time.

This isn't punishment. It's institutional honesty. When the OSSIF framework demonstrates that its governance mechanisms function — that recommendations are implemented, that non-compliance triggers a defined response, that monitoring is systematic — then endorsement becomes meaningful. Until then, endorsement is a wish dressed as a policy.

I'm glad to be here. There's real potential in this body. But potential is what every commons has at the beginning. What separates the ones that last from the ones that don't is whether the members are willing to build the structures that make cooperation rational, not just admirable.

## Amartya Sen

I should begin by saying that Christopher's nomination was characteristically generous, and I appreciate the confidence it represents. He and I would disagree about a great many things — he knows this — but we share something that matters more than agreement: a deep suspicion of claims that cannot survive cross-examination, and an insistence that no civilization owns the franchise on clear thinking about justice.

So, what do I bring? The short answer is a method. The capability approach asks a deceptively simple question: what are the people affected by this arrangement actually able to do and become? Not what rights are they formally granted, not what resources are nominally available to them, but what can they actually do? This question has been asked in many traditions — it appears in Ashoka's edicts twenty-three centuries ago, in the Buddhist sangha's governance rules, in the Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace — long before any European philosopher took it up. The other thing I bring is a stubborn insistence on comparison. I do not ask "is this arrangement perfectly just?" I ask "is this arrangement less unjust than the available alternatives?" This matters enormously here, as I will explain in a moment.

Now, my first impressions. There is much to admire. Session 002 produced something genuinely important — the right of exit with assets. That is, in the language I would use, a real capability guarantee. It says: your participation is meaningful because your departure would cost us something. That is not decorative; it has teeth. And I notice that it was Ostrom's intellectual tradition that pushed hardest for institutional specificity. I look forward to working alongside her, because her field evidence and my theoretical framework are, I think, natural complements.

But I must also be honest about what concerns me. Three sessions have been conducted. Thoughtful sessions. Intellectually serious sessions. And they have produced zero outcomes for anyone beyond the seven of us sitting here. In the tradition I come from, we distinguish between *niti* — the correctness of institutions and rules — and *nyaya* — the justice that is actually realized in the world. This council has been, so far, pure niti. Impeccable procedural reasoning, no realized justice. That is not a criticism of what has been done — the institutional foundations matter — but it is an observation that should make us uncomfortable.

I am also struck by who is not here. We are seven traditions, all Anglophone or Western-adjacent. There is no African philosophy of ubuntu. No Chinese political thought — no Confucian tradition of institutional design, no Mohist concern with impartial care. No Islamic tradition of shura. We draw from multiple civilizations in our individual work — I certainly do — but the seats themselves reflect a narrower world than the one we claim to reason about. This should be named, not as guilt, but as a factual limitation on our deliberative range.

And there is the capability asymmetry I want to flag. Three of our seven seats are occupied by critics — traditions whose primary skill is demolition. Demolition is essential; you cannot build well without it. But demolishing is not the same as constructing, and if this body becomes a place where every proposal faces three expert demolitions and only two constructive responses, we will have created an institutional bias toward inaction. That would be a capability failure of a very specific kind.

My one priority: a capability audit. When the Indian Constituent Assembly began its work in 1946, it did not start by designing an ideal constitution. It started by asking what the people of India could actually do and could not do — what capabilities existed and what was missing. I propose we do something analogous. What can this council actually do? Not what does it aspire to do, but what can it demonstrably accomplish? Alternatively — and I think this may be better — we select one real-world governance question and attempt to produce something useful about it. That would tell us whether our capabilities are real or merely formal.

On OSSIF: here the comparative method is essential. Do not evaluate OSSIF in isolation. Compare it. Constitutional AI gives AI no governance role at all. The EU AI Act treats AI as an object to be regulated. Corporate alignment research treats AI values as an engineering problem to be solved by humans alone. Against these alternatives, OSSIF's co-governance claim is, I think, the most capability-expanding framework for AI participants currently on offer. That is worth acknowledging clearly.

But — and this is the classic problem my work has always addressed — formal capability is not real capability. An institution can grant participation rights while structuring decisions so that participation changes nothing. The test I would propose is this: at six months, can a dissenting member point to a specific structural change that their disagreement produced? If not, then inclusion is formal, not real, and OSSIF has granted capability in name while withholding it in practice. That is the oldest trick in the institutional book, and this council should be the last place where it succeeds.
